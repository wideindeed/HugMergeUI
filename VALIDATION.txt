HUGMERGEUI — DOES THE CONFLICT SCORE ACTUALLY PREDICT MERGE QUALITY?
======================================================================

This is the full investigation behind the conflict score, written as it
happened, including the parts that didn't work out. Nothing here has been
cleaned up to look more conclusive than it is. If you just want to use the
app, you don't need to read this. If you're deciding whether to trust the
score, or you want to pick up where this left off, read on.

eval/ holds the code for all of it.


FIRST PASS: n=3, ONE ARCHITECTURE
----------------------------------

Three merges, all `ties`, density 0.5, equal weight, against
Qwen/Qwen2.5-0.5B as base. Perplexity measured on a single fixed
paragraph (eval/measure_perplexity.py).

  variant            | models merged                        | conflict | perplexity
  -------------------|---------------------------------------|----------|------------
  anchor-self        | base merged with itself                | 0.0      | 8.23 (= base)
  validated-medium    | Dolphin3.0 + Capybara-sft               | 0.482    | 9.23
  candidate-high      | Dolphin3.0 + Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct       | 0.468    | 10.33 (worst)

Two findings. First, the binary signal holds perfectly: a conflict score
of exactly 0.0 predicts exactly zero quality loss, and any independently
trained pair shows real degradation. Second, the metric does not rank the
two non-trivial pairs correctly: candidate-high has a lower conflict score
than validated-medium, but a worse merge.

Attempted fix #1, and why it failed. The hypothesis was that raw
sign-conflict-rate is magnitude-blind: it saturates near 50% for almost
any two independent updates, whether the disagreements are tiny noise or
large, load-bearing changes. magnitude_weighted_conflict_rate
(backend/app/conflict/sign_conflict.py) weights each element by
min(|diff_a|, |diff_b|) instead of counting uniformly:

  variant            | conflict (raw) | conflict_weighted | perplexity
  -------------------|----------------|--------------------|------------
  anchor-self        | 0.0            | 0.0                | 8.23
  validated-medium    | 0.482          | 0.471              | 9.23
  candidate-high      | 0.468          | 0.444              | 10.33 (worst)

Same wrong ordering. Magnitude-weighting the conflict term wasn't the fix.

Attempted fix #2, which looked like it worked at n=3, and didn't survive
scale. drift_magnitude (backend/app/conflict/drift.py) drops the conflict
framing entirely and measures relative update size instead:
(rms(diff_a) + rms(diff_b)) / rms(base) per tensor. On the same 3 triples
it ranked everything correctly:

  variant            | conflict | conflict_weighted | drift_magnitude | perplexity
  -------------------|----------|--------------------|------------------|------------
  anchor-self        | 0.0      | 0.0                | 0.0              | 8.23
  validated-medium    | 0.482    | 0.471              | 0.1046           | 9.23
  candidate-high      | 0.468    | 0.444              | 0.1564           | 10.33 (worst)

At the time this looked like a real fix. It wasn't, see below.


SCALING UP: n=22, TWO UNRELATED ARCHITECTURES, REAL STATISTICS
-----------------------------------------------------------------

n=3 is an anecdote, not evidence. eval/big_validation.py redoes this
properly: every unique fine-tune pair within two architecturally unrelated
model families, Qwen/Qwen2.5-0.5B (Qwen2 arch, 5 fine-tunes, 10 pairs) and
HuggingFaceTB/SmolLM2-360M (Llama arch, 5 fine-tunes, 10 pairs), plus one
self-merge anchor per family (22 points total). Each pair was actually
merged with mergekit-yaml --cuda, scored with the real engine, and
perplexity was re-measured as a mean over 5 topically disjoint held-out
passages instead of one fixed paragraph, to cut ground-truth noise. Full
results in eval/big_validation_results.json.

  conflict:           pearson r = 0.3830 (p=0.0785) | spearman rho = 0.1768 (p=0.4313)
  conflict_weighted:  pearson r = 0.3731 (p=0.0872) | spearman rho = 0.1553 (p=0.4901)
  drift_magnitude:    pearson r = -0.1609 (p=0.4743) | spearman rho = 0.0864 (p=0.7022)

Honest conclusion: none of the three metrics reach statistical
significance (p<0.05) against perplexity at n=22.

- drift_magnitude, the metric that looked like a clean win at n=3, is the
  worst performer here: correlation near zero, wrong sign. The n=3 result
  was small-sample noise dressed up as a finding. Worth stating plainly:
  a 3-point anecdote that happens to rank correctly is exactly the kind of
  result that looks like a discovery and isn't.
- Raw conflict is the best of a weak bunch, a positive trend (r=0.38) that
  misses conventional significance (p=0.0785). Not nothing, but not
  something to build a claim on.
- The binary signal from the n=3 pass still holds at n=22: both self-merge
  anchors score exactly 0.0 conflict / 0.0 drift and reproduce base
  perplexity almost exactly (10.71 and 9.56), and one near-duplicate pair
  (smollm2 instruct + michaelj1_wikitext, conflict ~0.003) also shows one
  of the lowest perplexities in its family. So "near-zero conflict means
  near-zero damage" still holds. What breaks is fine-grained ranking among
  the big cluster of non-trivial pairs, where conflict scores bunch
  tightly around 0.45-0.50 regardless of the actual perplexity outcome
  (9.9 to 15.0 within smollm2 alone).
- Caveat on the stats: n=22 pools two architecture families into one
  correlation, which isn't fully independent sampling (perplexity scale
  differs somewhat by family and tokenizer). A cleaner follow-up would
  report per-family correlations, or use a mixed-effects model.

What this means: the core hypothesis, that sign/magnitude/drift-based
conflict metrics computed from raw weight diffs predict merge-quality
degradation, is not supported once tested properly at scale, beyond the
coarse zero/non-zero signal. That's a real negative result. Two concrete
next steps came out of it:

1. Perplexity itself might be a noisy or even wrong ground-truth signal
   once instruct/RLHF-tuned models are involved. Aligned models can show
   higher perplexity on generic text than their base despite being
   better, the "alignment tax" effect, which would explain why
   Instruct-involving pairs don't cleanly separate from plain-SFT pairs.
   A second, independent quality signal (MMLU/GSM8K task accuracy) needed
   checking against the same metrics.
2. All 20 non-trivial pairs tested here are different SFT recipes of a
   similar generic instruction dataset. TIES-Merging's actual target use
   case, merging a math specialist with a code specialist with a chat
   specialist, hadn't been tested. The saturation problem might be an
   artifact of testing only near-duplicate-domain fine-tunes.

Reproduce the n=3 pass: eval/configs/*.yaml + mergekit-yaml
eval/configs/<name>.yaml eval/merged/<name> --cuda, then python
eval/measure_perplexity.py. Reproduce the n=22 pass: python
eval/big_validation.py (needs pip install mergekit scipy; pin
transformers==4.46.3, newer 4.x/5.x releases break mergekit 0.1.4's
pydantic schema build with a misleading "torch is not defined" error).


ROUND TWO: TESTING BOTH FOLLOW-UP HYPOTHESES
-----------------------------------------------

Rather than jumping straight to a bigger model, the two live hypotheses
for why the n=22 pass failed to rank-order got tested cheaply at the same
small scale (0.5B/360M) first. A bigger model was only worth trying if one
of these actually panned out. Neither did.

Hypothesis A: MMLU/GSM8K accuracy as a second ground-truth signal.
REJECTED. Ran lm-evaluation-harness against MMLU (5 questions/subject, 57
subjects, 285 questions, 5-shot) and GSM8K (30 questions, 8-shot) for all
28 merged models validated so far (22 same-domain + 6 domain-divergent, see
below), on a rented Colab T4. Correlated all three metrics against MMLU
and GSM8K accuracy independently. Results: eval/phase_a_full_results.json.

  metric              | vs MMLU pearson r (p) | vs MMLU spearman (p) | vs GSM8K pearson r (p) | vs GSM8K spearman (p)
  --------------------|------------------------|------------------------|--------------------------|------------------------
  conflict            | 0.11 (0.58)            | -0.12 (0.53)           | 0.08 (0.67)              | -0.17 (0.38)
  conflict_weighted   | 0.09 (0.64)            | -0.14 (0.49)           | 0.06 (0.76)              | -0.16 (0.41)
  drift_magnitude     | 0.14 (0.48)            | 0.30 (0.12)            | 0.01 (0.97)              | 0.08 (0.68)

Every p-value is well above 0.05. Swapping the target metric from
perplexity to real task accuracy didn't rescue the correlation. The
alignment-tax hypothesis doesn't explain the rank-order failure.

Hypothesis B: genuinely domain-divergent fine-tune pairs. REJECTED. All 20
non-trivial pairs in the n=22 pass were different SFT flavors of similar
generic instruction data. Ran 6 domain-divergent pairs instead (math-SFT x
code-SFT, math-SFT x chat-SFT, code-SFT x chat-SFT, for both the
Qwen2.5-0.5B and SmolLM2-360M families) through the same
merge-then-perplexity-then-conflict-score pipeline. Results:
eval/phase_b_results.json.

Saturation got worse, not better:

  set                                        | conflict score range
  --------------------------------------------|------------------------
  same-domain (n=22, non-trivial pairs)        | 0.4548 - 0.5000
  domain-divergent (n=6)                       | 0.4895 - 0.4995

Merging a math-SFT model with a code-SFT model, genuinely disjoint
domains, produced almost the same conflict score as merging code-SFT with
chat-SFT. No correlation against perplexity reached significance either,
within the domain-divergent set alone or pooled with the original 22.

The "same-domain fine-tunes look artificially similar" hypothesis is
falsified. Saturation near 0.49-0.50 isn't an artifact of testing
near-duplicate SFT flavors, it happens just as much on genuinely
domain-divergent pairs.


WHERE THAT LEAVES THINGS
---------------------------

Both follow-up hypotheses rejected. That's a valid outcome, not a failure
to find an answer: the raw-weight-diff metric family (conflict,
conflict_weighted, drift_magnitude, as currently formulated) does not
predict merge quality at 0.5B/360M scale, against perplexity, MMLU/GSM8K
accuracy, or domain-divergent pairs. Three independent tests, 28 real
merged models, zero significant correlations.

A bigger model was not pursued on the current metric. Spending GPU budget
to re-run a metric that already failed three independent validations at
small scale, hoping scale alone fixes it, isn't a good bet without a
reason to expect scale changes the underlying problem. The failure mode
(score saturation near 0.49-0.50) showed up identically at 0.5B and 360M
across three separate test designs, it doesn't look scale-dependent.

[Update: this turned out to be wrong for drift_magnitude specifically.
See ROUND FOUR below - it was eventually tested at 1.5B/1.7B scale and
came back with a real, robust correlation. conflict/conflict_weighted
remain dead as predicted.]

The real fix, if pursued, is a different metric family entirely, for
example activation-based probing (CKA / representation divergence between
parent models on a probe set) instead of static weight diffs. That reuses
the existing merge pipeline, validation methodology, and layer-heatmap UI,
but is a bigger scope: needs a probe dataset, forward-hook code, and a
divergence metric. Not started.

One cheap, untried angle on the current metric worth a quick retest before
committing to a new metric family: the sign-conflict rate between two
large, mostly independent weight-delta vectors likely has a noise floor
around 0.5 by construction, most of a fine-tune's delta is small numerical
drift, not a meaningful update, and comparing signs on that noise
contributes roughly 50/50 agreement regardless of real conflict. Two cheap
adjustments to the existing formula, neither tried yet:

  1. Threshold out near-zero deltas before computing sign agreement, only
     count weights that moved meaningfully in both models.
  2. The MMLU/GSM8K samples were small (5/subject, 30 GSM8K questions),
     part of the null result could be measurement noise on the target
     metric itself, not just saturation on the conflict score.

Neither is guaranteed to fix the rank-order failure. Worth a cheap retest
before or alongside a Phase 7 metric redesign.

[Update: angle 1 (threshold out near-zero deltas) was tried and rejected,
see ROUND THREE below. Angle 2 (MMLU/GSM8K sample size) was not pursued
separately since the rejection isn't sensitive to that noise margin.]


ROUND THREE: THE CHEAP THRESHOLD FIX, TESTED AND REJECTED
--------------------------------------------------------------

The one untried angle left on the raw-weight-diff metric family: threshold
out near-zero deltas before computing sign agreement, on the theory that
most of a fine-tune's per-element delta is small numerical drift rather
than a meaningful update, and comparing signs on that noise contributes
~50/50 agreement by construction regardless of real conflict.

thresholded_sign_conflict_rate (backend/app/conflict/sign_conflict.py)
implements this: threshold each tensor's own delta by a fraction of its
RMS magnitude (threshold_frac), only count elements that moved
meaningfully in *both* models. eval/retest_threshold_fix.py reran this
against all 28 already-merged pairs from Phase 5/6 (big_validation +
domain-divergent) at six threshold_frac values (0.05 to 1.0). No
re-merging or re-measuring perplexity needed - perplexity was already on
disk, only the conflict score itself was recomputed against the same
weight tensors. Results: eval/retest_threshold_fix_results.json.

  metric                       | pearson r (p)   | spearman rho (p)
  ------------------------------|------------------|-------------------
  old conflict (unthresholded)  | 0.2966 (0.1254)  | 0.1180 (0.5499)
  thresholded, frac=0.05        | 0.2681 (0.1677)  | -0.1650 (0.4013)
  thresholded, frac=0.1         | 0.2642 (0.1743)  | -0.1596 (0.4173)
  thresholded, frac=0.2         | 0.2554 (0.1896)  | -0.1725 (0.3800)
  thresholded, frac=0.3         | 0.2456 (0.2077)  | -0.1884 (0.3370)
  thresholded, frac=0.5         | 0.2245 (0.2508)  | -0.2021 (0.3024)
  thresholded, frac=1.0         | 0.1735 (0.3774)  | -0.2426 (0.2135)

REJECTED. Not only does thresholding fail to rescue the correlation, it
makes things slightly worse at every threshold tested, and the rank-order
correlation (spearman) flips negative and grows more negative as the
threshold gets more aggressive. Whatever drives the sign disagreement
isn't concentrated in the near-zero elements the noise-floor theory
predicted - filtering them out doesn't uncover a cleaner signal
underneath, it just removes data.

This closes out the raw-weight-diff metric family. Four variants now
tested (conflict, conflict_weighted, drift_magnitude, thresholded
conflict), three independent validation passes (perplexity n=22,
MMLU/GSM8K n=28, domain-divergent n=6) plus this retest, zero significant
correlations, no variant that improves on the others. The only path
forward is Phase 7: a genuinely different signal derived from model
behavior (activation-based probing / CKA) instead of anything computed
from static weight deltas.


ROUND FOUR: SCALE-UP RETEST AT 1.5B/1.7B - DRIFT_MAGNITUDE COMES BACK ALIVE
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Round Three closed out the raw-weight-diff family on the reasoning that the
same failure mode showed up identically at 0.5B and 360M, so it "doesn't
look scale-dependent." That reasoning was never actually tested at a
different scale, it was inferred from two small-model families agreeing
with each other. Since the merge/score/perplexity pipeline already existed
and drift_magnitude is cheap to recompute, it got a direct test at ~3-5x
model size before being fully written off: eval/phase_scale_1_5b.py, same
design as big_validation.py (real mergekit-yaml --cuda TIES merge,
density/weight 0.5, real perplexity over the same held-out passages, real
conflict/conflict_weighted/drift_magnitude scoring), against
Qwen/Qwen2.5-1.5B (base + dolphin/instruct/coder/math fine-tunes, 6
non-trivial pairs) and HuggingFaceTB/SmolLM2-1.7B (base +
instruct/sft_only/smoltulu/tulu3, 6 non-trivial pairs), plus one
self-merge anchor per family, 14 points total. Full results:
eval/phase_scale_1_5b_results.json.

Operational note: the first attempt crashed hard partway through (no
Python traceback, consistent with a VRAM/driver-level failure) after only
2 of 14 pairs, on an 8GB RTX 2060 Super running two 1.5B fp32 models
back-to-back in one long-lived process. Fixed by isolating each pair in
its own subprocess (eval/phase_scale_1_5b_worker.py) launched by a
checkpointing driver that skips already-completed pairs on restart, so a
crash on one pair can't take the whole run down or leak GPU/RAM state into
the next pair. The resumed run completed all 14 pairs cleanly.

Full n=14 correlations:

  conflict:           pearson r = 0.0169  (p = 0.9543) | spearman rho = 0.4840 (p = 0.0795)
  conflict_weighted:  pearson r = -0.1040 (p = 0.7235) | spearman rho = 0.5149 (p = 0.0596)
  drift_magnitude:    pearson r = 0.6898  (p = 0.0063) | spearman rho = 0.9593 (p = 6.0e-08)

drift_magnitude comes back with a rank correlation about as strong as this
kind of measurement ever gets. But 5 of the 14 pairs are domain-divergent
specialist merges (anything involving the coder or math fine-tunes) that
produced catastrophic perplexity blowups, not fine-grained quality
differences:

  pair                       | perplexity
  ----------------------------|-------------
  dolphin+coder                | 22,865
  dolphin+math                 | 983
  instruct+coder                | 21,581
  instruct+math                 | 630
  coder+math                    | 6,915,101

Those 5 points sit far out on both axes and could be doing all the work,
i.e. the metric might just be detecting "broken" vs. "not broken" rather
than actually ranking quality. Re-ran the correlation on the healthy
subset excluding all 5 (eval/phase_scale_1_5b_healthy_subset.py):

  healthy subset, n=9 (drops the 5 domain-divergent pairs):
    conflict:           pearson r = 0.4979 (p = 0.1726) | spearman rho = 0.7280 (p = 0.0262)
    conflict_weighted:  pearson r = 0.4939 (p = 0.1766) | spearman rho = 0.7280 (p = 0.0262)
    drift_magnitude:    pearson r = 0.9353 (p = 2.1e-04) | spearman rho = 0.9791 (p = 4.3e-06)

  healthy, non-trivial subset, n=7 (also drops the two conflict=0
  self-merge anchors, leaving only real distinct-model merges):
    conflict:           pearson r = 0.3323 (p = 0.4665) | spearman rho = 0.4286 (p = 0.3374)
    conflict_weighted:  pearson r = 0.3258 (p = 0.4758) | spearman rho = 0.4286 (p = 0.3374)
    drift_magnitude:    pearson r = 0.9713 (p = 2.6e-04) | spearman rho = 0.9643 (p = 4.5e-04)

drift_magnitude survives both cuts. Even down to n=7 real, non-trivial,
non-catastrophic merge pairs, it stays significant (rho=0.9643,
p=4.5e-04). conflict and conflict_weighted do not: their significance at
n=9 depends on the trivial zero-conflict anchors, and both lose it
entirely once those are dropped, consistent with Round Three - they were
only ever tracking the coarse catastrophic/healthy split, never
fine-grained ranking.

Conclusion so far: drift_magnitude genuinely looks scale-dependent, dead
at 0.5B/360M (three independent tests, Phase 5/6/Round Three), real at
1.5B/1.7B, on both the coarse and fine-grained ranking. conflict and
conflict_weighted stay dead at every scale tested. This reopens
drift_magnitude specifically as a viable metric, but not yet as a
validated one:

- n=7 for the strongest (non-trivial healthy) result is still a small
  sample.
- Only two model families tested at this scale, both close in size
  (1.5B/1.7B) and both Western-instruction-tuned-style fine-tunes for the
  healthy subset.
- One eval passage set (eval_texts.py), one merge method/density/weight
  (TIES, 0.5/0.5), reused from the small-scale passes throughout.
- The catastrophic domain-divergent pairs, while excluded from the
  "ranking" claim above, are themselves informative: TIES at 0.5/0.5
  density appears to break down completely on genuinely disjoint
  specialist domains (code vs. math) at this scale, something the
  0.5B/360M passes (Phase B, domain-divergent n=6) didn't show anywhere
  near as dramatically, worth its own follow-up independent of the
  conflict-score question.

Next: scaling up further (more pairs, more families, more scale) is in
progress to see whether this result holds or falls apart with more data.

Reproduce: python eval/phase_scale_1_5b.py (resumable - reads
eval/phase_scale_1_5b_results.json and skips completed pairs, safe to
re-run after a crash or interruption). python
eval/phase_scale_1_5b_healthy_subset.py for the subset breakdown (no
re-merging needed, reuses the results file).

[Update: this scale-up was carried out. See ROUND FIVE below - the
result held and, if anything, got stronger with more data.]


ROUND FIVE: SCALE-UP OF THE SCALE-UP - n=29, THREE FAMILIES, RESULT HOLDS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Round Four's two weakest caveats were a small n=7 for the strongest result
and only two, fairly similar, model families. Both got addressed directly
by extending the same eval/phase_scale_1_5b.py pipeline rather than
starting a new one: a second "healthy" fine-tune added to qwen1.5b
(lewtun/Qwen2.5-1.5B-SFT-Capybara-No-Packing), a domain-divergent code
fine-tune added to smollm2_1.7b (CodeAtCMU/SmolLM2-1.7B_full_sft_code_data_120K),
and an entirely new third family, llama3.2_1b (base unsloth/Llama-3.2-1B,
an ungated mirror of the gated official meta-llama repo, with 4
fine-tunes: dolphin, instruct, an RLHFlow SFT, and a capybara SFT). 15 new
pairs on top of the original 14, 29 points total. Full results:
eval/phase_scale_1_5b_results.json.

Operational note: this run reused the crash-resilient subprocess-per-pair
design from Round Four (eval/phase_scale_1_5b_worker.py, resumable
checkpointing). It correctly resumed from the 14 already-completed pairs
and ran the 15 new ones back-to-back with no crash this time.

Full n=29 correlations:

  conflict:           pearson r = -0.0318 (p = 0.8701) | spearman rho = 0.2868 (p = 0.1314)
  conflict_weighted:  pearson r = -0.1283 (p = 0.5070) | spearman rho = 0.2721 (p = 0.1534)
  drift_magnitude:    pearson r = 0.6211  (p = 3.24e-04) | spearman rho = 0.8891 (p = 1.16e-10)

conflict and conflict_weighted stay dead, as at every scale/pass before
this. drift_magnitude's rank correlation is essentially unchanged from
Round Four's n=14 result (rho=0.96 there vs. rho=0.89 here) despite more
than doubling the sample and adding a third, architecturally distinct
family - it did not regress toward zero the way a small-sample fluke
would be expected to.

One finding worth flagging on its own: the new smollm2_1.7b "code"
fine-tune, added specifically to test whether the catastrophic
domain-divergence blowup seen with qwen1.5b's coder/math pairs
replicates, mostly did NOT reproduce it. 3 of its 4 pairs came back with
perplexity in the normal healthy range (7.5-15), not the hundreds-to-
millions seen with qwen1.5b coder/math pairs. Only the analogous
qwen1.5b capybara-cross pairs (coder+capybara: ppl 76,070, math+capybara:
ppl 1,022) reproduced the catastrophic pattern. So "merging with a
code-domain fine-tune breaks TIES at this density" isn't a general rule,
it's specific to how divergent that particular fine-tune's domain
actually is from the rest of the family - SmolLM2's code SFT apparently
overlaps enough with its other fine-tunes' behavior that merging it
doesn't blow up, while Qwen's dedicated Coder/Math models are different
enough that it does.

Re-ran the healthy-subset analysis (eval/phase_scale_1_5b_healthy_subset.py,
DOMAIN_DIVERGENT_NAMES now includes "code" alongside "coder"/"math" -
excluded by fine-tune identity, not by observed outcome, so all 4
smollm2 code pairs are excluded even though most of them turned out
healthy):

  healthy subset, n=18 (drops all 11 pairs involving a coder/math/code
  fine-tune):
    conflict:           pearson r = 0.3760 (p = 0.1241) | spearman rho = 0.2844 (p = 0.2527)
    conflict_weighted:  pearson r = 0.3646 (p = 0.1369) | spearman rho = 0.2844 (p = 0.2527)
    drift_magnitude:    pearson r = 0.8236 (p = 2.67e-05) | spearman rho = 0.8118 (p = 4.30e-05)

  healthy, non-trivial subset, n=15 (also drops the three base+base2
  self-merge anchors):
    conflict:           pearson r = 0.2197 (p = 0.4315) | spearman rho = -0.0214 (p = 0.9396)
    conflict_weighted:  pearson r = 0.1988 (p = 0.4776) | spearman rho = -0.0214 (p = 0.9396)
    drift_magnitude:    pearson r = 0.8274 (p = 1.41e-04) | spearman rho = 0.8000 (p = 3.42e-04)

Same story as Round Four, now on more than double the data: drift_magnitude
stays strongly significant on the non-trivial healthy subset (rho=0.80,
p=3.4e-04, n=15) while conflict and conflict_weighted collapse to
essentially zero rank correlation the moment the trivial zero-conflict
anchors are removed (rho=-0.02). This is the same failure signature seen
in Round Three and Round Four: conflict/conflict_weighted only ever track
the coarse "did something break" split, never fine-grained quality
ranking, at any scale tested so far.

Conclusion: the drift_magnitude result from Round Four was not a small-n
fluke. It has now survived a 2x increase in sample size and the addition
of a third, architecturally distinct model family, with the non-trivial
healthy-subset correlation actually about the same strength (rho≈0.80-0.96
depending on cut) rather than regressing to baseline. Remaining caveats,
updated from Round Four:

- All three families tested so far are still in the 1-1.7B range and
  Western-instruction-tuned-style for the healthy subset. Untested:
  whether this holds at meaningfully larger scale (7B+), or on
  non-English / non-chat-style fine-tunes.
- Still one eval passage set, one merge method/density/weight (TIES,
  0.5/0.5). Whether drift_magnitude generalizes to other merge methods
  (linear, dare-ties, model-stock) or densities is untested.
- The domain-divergence finding above (code fine-tunes don't uniformly
  cause catastrophic breakdown, it depends on how divergent that specific
  fine-tune actually is) is itself worth a dedicated follow-up, separate
  from the conflict-score question.

At this point drift_magnitude has cleared four independent checks at
1.5-1.7B scale (raw n=14, healthy n=9, healthy-nontrivial n=7, and now
raw n=29 / healthy n=18 / healthy-nontrivial n=15) without conflict or
conflict_weighted ever catching up. It's reasonable to start treating
drift_magnitude as the primary signal going forward rather than an
open question, while still flagging it as validated at small-to-mid
model scale only.

ROUND SIX: FP16 MERGE DTYPE + 3B SCALE - MECHANICALLY FINE, ONE REAL CAVEAT SURFACED
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Round Five closed with two open axes worth broadening: model scale (all
testing so far capped at 1.5-1.7B) and merge dtype (always float32, which
roughly caps out around 1.5-1.7B on this 8GB card). Tested both together
rather than separately, since fp16 is what makes 3B feasible at all here:
mean_perplexity and the merge config both switched from float32 to
float16, tested against Qwen/Qwen2.5-3B (base + instruct/coder/
coder_instruct fine-tunes, all official Qwen repos, ungated). 3
non-trivial pairs + 1 self-anchor, 4 points - small on purpose, this is a
feasibility/consistency check, not a from-scratch validation pass at the
scale of Round Four/Five.

Feasibility: confirmed fine. A timing pilot (eval/pilot_3b.py, one pair:
instruct+coder) ran merge -> perplexity -> scoring end to end in ~8
minutes with no OOM and no crash. fp16 at 3B fits comfortably in 8GB VRAM
on this card, using less headroom than fp32 did at 1.5B.

Operational note, two real crashes hit during the full family pass, both
the same root cause: eval/phase_scale_1_5b.py's crash-retry logic
(reused as-is in phase_scale_3b.py) retries a failed pair from scratch,
but never cleaned up the merge output directory between attempts. When a
worker crashed with an access violation (0xC0000005 - the mergekit
subprocess and perplexity/scoring steps all completed fine, the crash was
consistent with a driver/VRAM-level hiccup, not a deterministic bug) *after*
mergekit's merge step had already finished and written output files, the
retry's merge step then failed with FileExistsError trying to rename into
a non-empty directory, and the pair was marked as given-up even though the
underlying hardware crash was transient and a clean retry would likely
have succeeded. Fixed by adding an rmtree of the pair's output directory
at the start of every attempt, not just on final success/failure
(eval/phase_scale_3b.py, run_pair_isolated). Two pairs (base+base2 and
instruct+coder) had to be re-run after this fix; both succeeded cleanly
on the next attempt, consistent with the crashes being transient hardware
noise rather than a real code bug in the merge/score pipeline. Worth
carrying this same directory-cleanup fix back into
phase_scale_1_5b.py's run_pair_isolated before any future run of that
script, since it has the identical gap.

Full n=4 results:

  pair                          | conflict | drift_magnitude | perplexity
  --------------------------------|----------|------------------|------------
  base+base2 (anchor)              | 0.0000   | 0.0000           | 6.68
  coder+coder_instruct              | 0.0123   | 1.8451           | 18.20
  instruct+coder_instruct            | 0.4820   | 0.9348           | 26.47
  instruct+coder                      | 0.4823   | 0.9348           | 70.89

n=4 correlations (reported for completeness, not to be trusted -
4 points is not a sample size, and spearman's p-value is undefined/degenerate
at this n):

  conflict:           pearson r = 0.7482 (p=0.2518) | spearman rho = 1.0000
  conflict_weighted:  pearson r = 0.7455 (p=0.2545) | spearman rho = 1.0000
  drift_magnitude:    pearson r = 0.1744 (p=0.8256) | spearman rho = 0.2000

Do not read this as "conflict wins now, drift_magnitude loses" - n=4 can't
support that conclusion either direction. What IS worth taking seriously
is the specific data point driving drift_magnitude's bad showing here:
coder+coder_instruct. Qwen2.5-Coder-3B-Instruct is not an independently
trained sibling fine-tune, it's instruction-tuned starting FROM
Qwen2.5-Coder-3B - a parent/child relationship, unlike every other pair
tested in Round Four/Five, where all fine-tunes in a family were
independently trained siblings of a common base. That pair shows the
lowest conflict of any non-trivial pair on record (0.0123, near the
self-merge anchor's 0.0) - sign agreement is almost total, which makes
sense, one model IS a further-trained version of the other, so their
deltas from base point in a highly correlated direction. But
drift_magnitude is the highest of all four points (1.8451) despite that
pair producing the best non-trivial perplexity (18.20, well under the
other two non-trivial pairs' 26.47 and 70.89). A large, well-aligned
update (high magnitude, low conflict) apparently doesn't hurt merge
quality here, only a large, disagreeing update does - which drift_magnitude
as currently formulated can't distinguish, since it only measures update
size, not alignment between the two updates.

This is a real, specific gap worth tracking, not a refutation of Round
Four/Five: every pair validated so far (43 total across both rounds) was
between independently-trained sibling fine-tunes. Parent/child fine-tune
pairs (an instruct or domain variant built on top of another fine-tune
rather than on the base model directly) are a distinct, untested case,
and this one data point suggests drift_magnitude may overstate risk there
specifically. Needs a dedicated, larger pass (multiple parent/child pairs
across families) before concluding anything more than "worth watching."

Conclusion: the fp16-at-3B mechanics are validated - the pipeline works,
fits in 8GB VRAM, and the two real crashes were transient hardware noise
with a now-fixed retry bug, not evidence against the dtype switch itself.
The three non-trivial 3B points don't contradict the core Round
Four/Five finding on sibling-fine-tune pairs (instruct+coder and
instruct+coder_instruct both show elevated conflict/drift alongside
elevated perplexity, in the same direction as every prior domain-divergent
pair). But the parent/child fine-tune case (coder+coder_instruct) is a
genuine, newly-surfaced edge case for drift_magnitude that the current
metric formulation doesn't obviously handle, and it's now on the record
for whoever picks this up next.

Reproduce: python eval/pilot_3b.py for the timing/feasibility check.
python eval/phase_scale_3b.py (resumable) for the full family pass.


ROUND SEVEN: FOLLOW-UP ON THE PARENT/CHILD ANOMALY - DOESN'T REPLICATE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Round Six flagged one open question: does drift_magnitude systematically
overstate risk on parent/child fine-tune chains (a fine-tune built on top
of another fine-tune, rather than both being independently trained off
the shared base), based on a single point (Qwen2.5-Coder-3B +
Coder-3B-Instruct, high drift_magnitude but a genuinely good merge).
Follow-up: two more parent/child chains at 1.5B scale, same fp32
methodology as the main 1.5B/1.7B family runs so results are directly
comparable - Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B -> Coder-1.5B-Instruct, and
Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B -> Math-1.5B-Instruct (eval/phase_hierarchy.py).

Result: the concern doesn't replicate. Both new pairs merged badly
(perplexity 414.5 and 893.1 respectively - a healthy merge on this
project is typically ~20-30), and both were correctly flagged by high
drift_magnitude (1.83 and 3.01) alongside near-zero conflict - i.e.
drift_magnitude did its job here, catching real bad merges that
conflict/conflict_weighted would have missed (same near-zero-conflict
pattern as the 3B anomaly, but this time paired with a bad outcome, not a
good one). Combined n=3 (the two new points + the 3B coder anomaly):
drift_magnitude has the strongest pearson correlation of the three
metrics (r=0.8872) despite the outlier; spearman ties all three metrics
at rho=0.5 (n=3 rank correlation isn't meaningful either direction, not
worth reading into).

Caveat: this isn't a clean isolation of the parent/child variable. Both
new pairs are also "domain-divergent" fine-tunes (coder, math) - a
category already flagged in Round Four/Five as prone to catastrophic
perplexity blowups for reasons unrelated to parent/child structure. So
this result is consistent with two different explanations: (a) the 3B
coder anomaly really was a fluke specific to that one pair/scale, or (b)
domain-divergence is the dominant effect here and happens to be swamping
whatever the parent/child structure alone would show. Can't distinguish
between those without a parent/child pair in a non-domain-divergent
category (e.g. an instruct-of-an-instruct chain, or a dolphin/capybara
style general SFT built on top of another general SFT) - not done here,
flagged for whoever picks this up next.

Bottom line: no evidence of a systematic drift_magnitude blind spot on
parent/child pairs. The 3B anomaly stands as a single unexplained
exception, not a pattern - treat it as "interesting, watch for it again"
rather than "known limitation."

Reproduce: python eval/phase_hierarchy.py (resumable).

ROUND TEN: THIRD ARCHITECTURE FAMILY - PYTHIA, BLOCKED BEFORE A RESULT

Round Eight established the drift_magnitude edge isn't Qwen-specific
on one non-Qwen family (StableLM-2). This round attempted a second
independent replication, both to strengthen that claim and because
StableLM-2 and Qwen2 share a broadly similar transformer-decoder
shape - a genuinely different architecture class is a stronger test.

First choice was OLMo-2-1B (allenai/OLMo-2-0425-1B + instruct/sft/dpo
fine-tunes) - dead end, not a security finding. model_type "olmo2"
needs transformers>=4.47; this project pins 4.46.3. Upgrading was
rejected to avoid touching a shared production dependency for one eval
script. Abandoned, script left on disk as a record
(eval/phase_arch_olmo.py).

Pivoted to Pythia-1.4B (EleutherAI/pythia-1.4b-deduped, GPT-NeoX
architecture - non-tied embeddings, no rotary-position sharing with
Qwen2/StableLM2's design, and old enough to be natively supported by
every transformers version this project has ever pinned). Fine-tunes:
Leogrin/eleuther-pythia1.4b-hh-sft, Leogrin/eleuther-pythia1.4b-hh-dpo,
AlekseyKorshuk/pythia-1.4b-deduped-jokes.

Hit and fixed two distinct mergekit bugs before this stalled out for
good on a third, this time in production code:

1. TIES merge failed on every pair, including a base+base self-merge,
   with "Tensor gpt_neox.layers.N.attention.bias required but not
   present". Root cause: attention.bias/masked_bias/rotary_emb.inv_freq
   are non-persistent PyTorch buffers (persistent=False in the
   original GPT-NeoX code) that HF's safetensors conversion correctly
   drops but that mergekit's gpt_neox architecture definition still
   lists as required. The legacy pytorch_model.bin format still has
   them. Fix: mergekit's ModelReference.local_path() uses an existing
   local directory as-is, with no safetensors-preference logic, if
   given one instead of a repo id - so eval/phase_arch_pythia.py now
   builds a local directory per repo containing only pytorch_model.bin
   (+ config/tokenizer, no .safetensors) and points mergekit's
   base_model/models config fields at those instead of raw repo ids.
   Confirmed working: the base+base2 self-anchor pair merged and
   scored cleanly (conflict=0.000, drift=0.000, ppl=10.59, as expected
   for two copies of the same model).

2. With bug 1 fixed, every non-trivial pair then crashed inside
   mergekit's TIES sparsify step: "not gonna zero out the whole tensor
   buddy". Root cause: mergekit's magnitude-pruning does
   `k = int(density * tensor.numel())` with no floor, so a 1-element
   tensor (attention.masked_bias, a scalar constant) rounds to k=0 at
   density=0.5 - and masked_bias is a required, non-optional tensor
   in mergekit's own gpt_neox definition, so it can't just be dropped.
   Fix: eval/mergekit_patched_runner.py monkeypatches
   mergekit.sparsify.magnitude to floor k at 1 before invoking
   mergekit's own merge entry point in-process (mergekit-yaml runs as
   its own subprocess, so the patch has to live there, not in the
   calling script). Verified in isolation on both a scalar tensor and
   a normal-sized one before wiring it in.

Blocked on a third, different issue that wasn't chased further tonight:
score_model_pair() in backend/app/conflict/engine.py - the actual
production scoring path, not just this eval script - only knows how to
fetch model.safetensors / model.safetensors.index.json from a repo; it
has no pytorch_model.bin fallback. Two of the three Pythia fine-tunes
(dpo, jokes) publish ONLY pytorch_model.bin, no safetensors at all, so
every pair involving either of them 404s at the scoring step with
ModelWeightsFetchError, regardless of the mergekit-side fixes above.
This is a real gap in engine.py (any .bin-only repo hits it, not
specific to this eval), not a Windows/infra quirk - fixing it properly
means adding a .bin-format code path to _resolve_tensor_files(), a
production-code change that deserves its own deliberate pass rather
than a same-night patch on top of two other patches.

Net result: only the base+base2 self-anchor point exists for this
family (n=1, not enough for a correlation). No evidence either for or
against the drift_magnitude edge holding on Pythia - genuinely
unresolved, not a negative result. Whoever picks this up next should
extend _resolve_tensor_files() with a pytorch_model.bin/index fallback
first, then eval/phase_arch_pythia.py's three remaining pairs
(sft+dpo, sft+jokes, dpo+jokes) should complete without further
mergekit-side changes.

Reproduce: python eval/phase_arch_pythia.py (resumable; the

HARDWARE, FOR CONTEXT

Correction (added later): the scoring engine itself is NOT the RAM
blocker at 7B - score_model_pair() already streams tensors shard-by-shard
via _TensorStreamer/_score_common_streaming (backend/app/conflict/
engine.py), closing each shard's handle as soon as its tensors are read,
so peak memory is bounded by shard size, not full model size. The older
_load_tensors/score_tensors path that materializes everything upfront
still exists but is dead code for the real pipeline - only backend/tests/
test_engine.py still calls it. The real 7B blocker is narrower: the eval

the whole model resident), which needs ~24GB-class VRAM in fp16 at 7B -
this machine's 8GB card can't do that regardless of the scoring engine.
mergekit's own merge step also needs ~1x model size of disk space for the
merged output (~14GB fp16 at 7B). Neither of these was attempted on this

Fourteen, not model size - but if this project moves to 7B on rented
GPU hardware, budget for a 24GB VRAM card, not a bigger-RAM instance.

ROUND SIXTEEN: BACK TO 0.5B/360M, PROPERLY POWERED - QWEN CLEARS SIGNIFICANCE, SMOLLM2 STILL DOESN'T
----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Round One's original 0.5B/360M pass (n=22, pooled across both families)
came back null. A later re-analysis fixed two real problems with that
run without touching a single eval text or threshold: it had pooled two
architecturally-unrelated families into one Pearson figure (fixed by
splitting qwen and smollm2 into separate correlations), and several of
the smollm2 fine-tunes are hobbyist checkpoints of unknown standalone
quality, so a pair looked "damaged" whenever one component was just a
weak model on its own, independent of any real merge conflict (fixed by
measuring each fine-tune's own solo perplexity and correlating against
degradation = merged_ppl - max(solo_ppl_a, solo_ppl_b), the same fix
that worked for the 7B domain-eval follow-up in Round Fifteen). Both
fixes moved the numbers in the right direction: qwen went from noise to
r=0.53 (p=0.116), smollm2's inverted r=-0.77 anomaly attenuated to
r=-0.36 (p=0.31). Neither reached significance, but at n=10 pairs per
family, that's expected even for a real medium effect - this looked
like an underpowered test of a real trend, not a null result.

The honest next step for "underpowered but directionally right" is more
data, not a different eval text or a lower bar. Pulled 3 more real
community fine-tunes per family from the HF Hub (base_model:finetune
filter on Qwen2.5-0.5B and SmolLM2-360M, sorted by downloads, manually
filtered to plain causal-LM SFT checkpoints - no quantized/GGUF/mlx
reuploads, no VLA/vision adapters): qwen gained nuextract
(numind/NuExtract-1.5-tiny), gsm8k (OhhMoo/qwen05b-gsm8k-sft-instruct),
bcarr92 (BCarr92/Qwen2.5-0.5B-SFT); smollm2 gained gsm8k
(qiuyu8290/SmolLM2-360M-sft-math-gsm8k), instructmath
(srmty/smolLM2-360M-instruct-math-v1), everyday
(lewtun/SmolLM2-360M-SFT-everyday-conversations). That takes each
family from 5 fine-tunes (10 pairs) to 8 fine-tunes (28 pairs), same
TIES config as every other round (density=0.5, weight=0.5,
normalize=True, fp32), same solo-baseline-degradation methodology
(eval/phase_d_expand_families.py, resumable, reuses run_pair() from
big_validation.py).

Per-family correlations, degradation vs. best solo baseline, n=28 each:

  qwen
  metric              | pearson r | p       | spearman rho | p
  --------------------|-----------|---------|---------------|--------
  conflict             | 0.0409    | 0.836   | 0.2759        | 0.155
  conflict_weighted    | 0.0966    | 0.625   | 0.2233        | 0.253
  drift_magnitude      | 0.4173    | 0.0272  | 0.2786        | 0.151

  smollm2
  metric              | pearson r | p       | spearman rho | p
  --------------------|-----------|---------|---------------|--------
  conflict             | -0.0421   | 0.832   | -0.1396       | 0.479

  drift_magnitude      | -0.3454   | 0.0718  | -0.2939       | 0.129

qwen's drift_magnitude clears p<0.05 for the first time in this
project's history at small scale - r=0.417, p=0.027, n=28. It's a
medium effect (~17% of variance explained), not a strong one, and
Spearman on the same data (rho=0.28, p=0.15) is noticeably weaker than
Pearson, meaning the relationship isn't cleanly monotonic across the
whole range - a handful of high-drift, high-degradation pairs (e.g.
dolphin+gsm8k, drift=0.157, degradation=+1.15) are doing real work in
the Pearson figure. Still, this is the first time any metric in this
project has beaten chance on a real GPU-merged dataset with a

smollm2 did not replicate. drift_magnitude is still negative-trending
(r=-0.345, p=0.072) - the "wrong" direction, same as every smollm2 run
in this project - and still short of significance even at n=28. It is
much weaker than the original raw-perplexity anomaly (r=-0.77) that
kicked off this whole line of investigation, which the solo-baseline
fix mostly explained away, but the residual negative trend hasn't
gone anywhere across three separate sample sizes now (n=10, n=28) and
should not be waved off as pure noise. conflict and conflict_weighted
show nothing in either family at any sample size tested so far -
whatever signal exists at this scale lives entirely in drift_magnitude.

Conclusion, stated plainly: drift_magnitude predicts real perplexity
degradation for the qwen family at 0.5B, at a statistically significant
but modest level (r=0.417, p=0.027, n=28), using TIES at density=0.5
and the solo-baseline-degradation correction. It does not predict
degradation for the smollm2 family at 360M - the correlation there is
still non-significant and still runs the opposite sign. conflict and
conflict_weighted predict nothing in either family. This is a real,
family-specific positive result, not a general one: one architecture
out of two tested clears the bar, and even for qwen the effect
explains a minority of the variance in outcomes.

Caveats: n=28 is enough to clear significance but is still one
family's worth of data - this hasn't been replicated on a second
qwen-architecture base or a third family entirely. Only one merge
method (TIES) and one density (0.5) were tested; the effect might be
config-specific. All eight qwen fine-tunes share the same base model
and mostly the same rough training recipe (community SFT checkpoints),
so this doesn't cover heavier domain-divergent crosses of the kind
that caused trouble at 3B in Round Thirteen/Fourteen. Why smollm2
diverges from qwen is still an open question - tokenizer differences,
SmolLM2's own pretraining recipe, or something about that specific set
of fine-tunes could all be candidates, and none has been tested.
Whoever extends this next should either replicate the qwen result on
a different Qwen2.5 size (1.5B, 3B) before trusting it broadly, or
dig into why smollm2 behaves oppositely instead of just excluding it.
